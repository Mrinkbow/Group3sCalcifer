Absurdism

Absurdism, a philosophical concept associated with thinkers like Albert Camus, explores the tension between human quest for meaning and the apparent meaninglessness of the universe. In the face of an indifferent cosmos, absurdism suggests that individuals must create their own meaning and purpose. Everyone sees this philosophy as depressing. The latter would be nihilism. This is exemplified in Camus' essay "The Myth of Sisyphus," where he argues for embracing the absurdity of life and finding meaning through acts of rebellion and creation.

Stoicism

Stoicism, an ancient philosophical school founded by Zeno of Citium, teaches that virtue and happiness result from living in harmony with nature and accepting life's challenges with equanimity. Stoicism emphasizes the dichotomy of control, asserting that individuals have power over their thoughts and actions, while external events are beyond their control. Practitioners aim for inner tranquility by cultivating virtues like wisdom and courage, and by focusing on what they can control, adapting to circumstances without being swayed by emotional reactions. Figures like Epictetus, Seneca, and Marcus Aurelius are central to Stoic philosophy.

Nihilism

Nihilism, it is a philosophical belief that rejects inherent meaning or value in life, proposing that existence lacks objective purpose. Advocates of nihilism often challenge traditional moral and social structures, asserting that values are arbitrary human constructs. Friedrich Nietzsche, while associated with nihilism, critiqued it, distinguishing between passive nihilism (denying meaning) and active nihilism (creating new values). Nihilistic perspectives often question established beliefs, arguing that reality is ultimately devoid of intrinsic significance.

Existentialism

This one is similar to Absurdism. Existentialism, a philosophical movement prominent in the 19th and 20th centuries, centers on individual existence, freedom, and choice. Existentialists, including Jean-Paul Sartre and Albert Camus, argue that individuals define their own essence through personal choices rather than adhering to predetermined identities. Themes of existential angst, authenticity, and the search for meaning in an apparently indifferent world are central to existentialist thought. Sartre's quote "existence precedes essence" encapsulates the movement's core principle. Which is just fancy speak for you exist first, but your actions define your very essence.
